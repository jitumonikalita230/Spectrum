The Expanding Universe of Participation:
Citizen Science in Astronomy and Astrophysics

Dr. Dhritimaan Gogoi
Dept of Physics, Pandu College

Long before the professional observatories and space agencies, ordinary people tracked the motions of the Sun, Moon, and stars to build calendars, navigate oceans, and interpret the cosmos. As such, for most of human history, astronomy can be considered a citizen science. Ancient skywatchers in civilizations from Mesopotamia to Mesoamerica recorded celestial events with astonishing precision. Table 1 presents a comprehensive timeline outlining the development of astronomy throughout human civilization. In the 16th Century, meticulous observations of Tycho Brahe, a Danish astronomer, laid the foundation of modern astrophysics. Later in the 18th and 19th centuries, the likes of William Herschel, Charles Messier, Caroline Herschel, William Parsons, and many others contributed to discovery, documentation, and photography using equipment built in their own backyards. In the 20th century, the field became increasingly professionalized, and with the advent of large telescopes, advanced detectors, and complex theoretical frameworks, it shifted the frontier of discovery. Simultaneously, universities and national observatories were established across nations. In the current 21st century, technological transformation spearheaded by Big Data, Artificial Intelligence, and Machine Learning has taken the forefront in analysis and research. Yet open data, distributed computing, and human ingenuity in pattern recognition have sparked a renaissance of public participation. Citizen science in astronomy and astrophysics has evolved into a powerful research paradigm, enabling non-professionals to contribute meaningfully to the study of exoplanets, galaxies, variable stars, supernovae, and even gravitational waves. This article explores the modern landscape of citizen science in astronomy and astrophysics: its historical roots, scientific impact, technological enablers, social implications, challenges, and future directions.

The Historical Continuum:

Astronomy has long bridged the divide between professionals and amateurs. The 1781 discovery of Uranus by William Herschel remains a landmark example of amateur achievement. In the 19th century, coordinated networks of skywatchers cataloged comets and variable stars, a tradition institutionalized by the American Association of Variable Star Observers (AAVSO), which formalized long-term stellar monitoring. Although the 20th century brought increasing professionalization, citizen astronomers continued contributing through comet discoveries, asteroid tracking, and transient observations. The rise of CCD technology, automated surveys, and space telescopes initially widened the technological gap. The digital revolution, however, transformed participation. Public data releases from projects like the Sloan Digital Sky Survey (SDSS) and the growth of high-speed internet enabled volunteers worldwide to analyze vast datasets. Citizen science reemerged as a vital force in modern, data-driven astrophysics.

Citizen Science:

A few of the areas where citizen science has contributed are highlighted in the following paragraphs:

The Zooniverse Model:

One of the most transformative developments in modern citizen science has been the creation of large-scale online platforms. Among them is Zooniverse, which was launched in 2009 with the Galaxy Zoo project. The Galaxy Zoo project itself was a major success, launched back in 2007. The ingenuity of the human mind excels at pattern recognition and detecting discrepancies. Even with the advancement in Artificial Intelligence, automated algorithms, and Image analysis, machines struggle with the detection of nuanced morphological features. Galaxy Zoo invites volunteers to classify images of galaxies obtained from SDSS. Before taking part in galaxy classification, volunteers complete a short and accessible training session that introduces them to the main morphological types—spiral, elliptical, irregular, merging, and related forms. The goal is not to overwhelm participants with technical detail, but to give them just enough guidance to recognize patterns confidently. The process itself is thoughtfully structured: volunteers follow simple tutorials, examine images one at a time, and complete small, clearly defined tasks within a larger distributed system. What makes the approach powerful is collective judgment. Each galaxy is classified independently by many participants, and their responses are combined. This reduces individual bias and random error, leading to results that are both reliable and reproducible. In this way, citizen science projects show that with thoughtful design and expert guidance, non-specialists can contribute data that meets professional scientific standards. The ease of use has facilitated and motivated amateur astronomers and volunteers to contribute towards the classification of millions of galaxy images. Within months, hundreds of thousands of volunteers contributed towards these classifications. The results obtained from such classification also led to peer-reviewed publications, which contributed to new scientific insights. For example, one of the major contributions was the identification of the “Green Peas” (GP) galaxies. GP’s are rare, compact, low-mass systems with extremely high star-formation rates. Similarly, in 2007, Hanny van Arkel, a Dutch schoolteacher, discovered the ‘cosmic ghost’ named as Hanny’s Voorwerp (Dutch for "Hanny's Object"), which is a unique, bright green, galaxy-sized gas cloud located ~650 million light-years away near spiral galaxy IC 2497. The success of this project demonstrates that non-experts, when properly guided, can perform tasks comparable in accuracy to trained astronomers.

With careful expert oversight and the aggregation of multiple independent classifications, researchers can ensure high reliability and strong statistical robustness in their results. Following the success of Galaxy Zoo, Zooniverse expanded into multiple astrophysical domains, most prominently exoplanet detection in light curves, gravitational lens identification, supernova classification, and radio galaxy morphology analysis.

Exoplanet Discovery:

Space missions such as NASA’s Kepler and TESS have monitored hundreds of thousands of stars, producing enormous collections of light curves. Light curves capture tiny, periodic dips in a star’s brightness that may signal a planet passing or transiting across its face. As of now, the search for exoplanets around other stars has become one of the most exciting frontiers in modern astrophysics. In order to analyze such light curves, scientists and researchers have developed automated algorithms. Using such algorithms, computers are remarkably efficient at scanning the sea of data and flagging potential signals of the presence of exoplanets. Yet complex patterns or combined light curves might mislead the algorithm. Additionally, perfect observations are also rare in scenarios where multiple data points are missing. Besides random stellar activity, instrumental noise, and subtle irregularities can mimic or obscure planetary signatures. Under such scenarios, human insight becomes invaluable. Citizen science platforms allow volunteers to visually inspect such light curves, which helps to confirm genuine planetary transits and spot unusual patterns that automated systems might miss. An example of such a project is Planet Hunters.

Fig 2: Planet Hunters interface for detecting exoplanets

Time-Domain Astronomy:

Astronomy is primarily a time-dependent study. Many of the most exciting events in the cosmos, such as supernovae, gamma-ray bursts, and tidal disruption events, appear suddenly and fade quickly. Capturing them requires fast identification and immediate follow-up observations. Organizations like the American Association of Variable Star Observers continue to coordinate dedicated amateur astronomers who monitor variable stars night after night.

Distributed Computing:

A few of the distributed computing projects harness the idle processing power of volunteers’ computers. Notable among the projects are: SETI@home (1999), developed by researchers at the University of California, Berkeley, who invited public participants to analyze radio signals in the search for extraterrestrial intelligence. Another project, Einstein@Home, analyzes data from gravitational wave detectors and radio telescopes, searching for pulsars and other signals. Volunteers’ computers perform computationally intensive analyses that would otherwise require dedicated supercomputers.

Near-Earth Objects:

Asteroid detection and tracking represent another domain where citizen scientists play a vital role. Organizations such as the International Astronomical Union coordinate reporting and naming conventions. Amateur contributions enhance planetary defense efforts by refining orbital parameters. In some cases, amateurs have discovered comets and minor planets that later received official designations. One such example is the Indian based amateur astronomer Amar Sharma, who discovered the asteroid “Sharma 380370”, named after him (inset Figure).

Social and Scientific Impact of Citizen Science:

Citizen science has done more than only generate valuable research—it has also reshaped how society connects with science. Contributing to such efforts is the “Open data” policies from agencies such as NASA and the European Space Agency that have made astronomical data accessible to anyone with an internet connection. Students, teachers, retirees, and enthusiasts across the globe can now work simultaneously with the same datasets used by professional astronomers. Access to online platforms further reduces geographic and economic barriers, allowing individuals in regions without major observatories to contribute meaningfully to the frontline research. Equally important is the sense of community these platform fosters. Volunteers are often motivated by curiosity and a desire to contribute to something larger than themselves. Various project forums are seen to frequently evolve into vibrant spaces where participants discuss and exchange ideas, celebrate discoveries together, while collaborating on the specific research. Oftentimes, this engagement translates into a concrete scientific output. Citizen science initiatives have produced hundreds of peer-reviewed publications, with volunteers often acknowledged or included as co-authors. As previously mentioned, Galaxy Zoo, for example, has numerous studies that explored galaxy morphology, star formation, and environmental influences. Likewise, participants in Planet Hunters have also helped confirm new exoplanets. The true strength of these contributions lies in their methodological rigor. By combining multiple independent classifications, applying statistical aggregation, calibrating results against expert analyses, and maintaining transparent workflows, citizen science projects ensure that their findings are both accurate and reproducible. Taken together, these practices demonstrate that citizen science is not merely inclusive and inspiring—it is also grounded in the same standards of reliability and scientific integrity that define professional research.

Challenges and Future Directions:

Despite its successes, as reported in this short article, citizen science faces several challenges in its implementation. Mainly, issues related to

Data Quality Control: For ensuring consistent and accurate classifications, the process or scientific output requires multiple independent assessments and careful statistical analysis.

Volunteer Retention: Engagement often declines over time. Sustaining participation requires thoughtful design and feedback mechanisms. A small study conducted by Dr. Bandana Das (2025) found that overall engagement levels were modest. However, volunteer retention was highest among participants who accessed projects through computers. In contrast, the greatest initial engagement came from users on mobile or other portable devices, although retention rates within this group were comparatively lower.

Several additional challenges—such as equity of access, recognition and credit, and ethical considerations—can be addressed through thoughtful planning and careful management. By implementing inclusive policies, ensuring fair acknowledgment of contributions, and maintaining transparency in data use and research goals, citizen science initiatives can reduce barriers and strengthen trust. Such deliberate efforts are essential for expanding sustaining meaningful and public participation in scientific research. The next decades will likely witness deeper integration of citizen science into astrophysical research. As telescopes grow more powerful and datasets more complex, the need for collaborative analysis will intensify. One such example is the Vera C. Rubin Observatory (inset Figure), which became operational in October 2025. It will conduct the Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST) and will generate tens of terabytes of data nightly. Although the automated systems will detect millions of transient events, citizen science platforms are expected to help filter, classify, and prioritize unusual phenomena.

Fig: Vera C. Rubin Observatory

Conclusion

In conclusion, the deep interconnectedness between citizen science and astronomy reflects the unique cultural place astronomy has held in human civilization since antiquity. Unlike laboratory sciences limited to specialized facilities, the night sky is a shared heritage—visible and accessible to all. Citizen science restores this historical connection, linking modern astrophysics with its roots in collective observation and curiosity. Today, citizen science is no longer a peripheral activity. It shows that exploring the universe is not the exclusive domain of professionals but a collaborative human endeavor. In this renewed partnership between experts and the public, astronomy and astrophysics are, in many ways, returning to their origins—sciences shaped by people, united beneath a common sky.

Exotic Hadrons

Pranami Baishya
Research Scholar, Gauhati University


Introduction:

For decades, scientists assumed that quarks, the fundamental building blocks of matter, could only unite in simple pairs or triplets. This exquisite image helped form our knowledge of the subatomic realm. However, it appears that nature is not restricted by simplicity. Tetraquarks and pentaquarks are exceptional particles composed of four and five quarks, demonstrating that matter may organize itself in considerably more complex ways than previously thought.

Their research sheds fresh light on the underlying dynamics of the strong force, implying that the more we delve into the cosmos, the richer and more fascinating its structure becomes. Exotic hadrons challenge and broaden the standard quark model. Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) allows for a wider range of particle configurations, including those with four or five quarks, whereas hadrons were previously thought to be either mesons (quark-antiquark pairs) or baryons (three quarks). The fundamental principle governing all observable particles in the subatomic realm is color neutrality, or being a "color singlet."

Discovery of Tetraquarks:

In 2003, the Belle Collaboration in Japan discovered a new particle known as X(3872), providing the first strong evidence for a Tetraquark. This particle was identified during high-energy electron-positron collisions. What distinguished X(3872) was that its attributes did not correspond to any known meson or baryon. Its mass, decay channels, and behavior indicated that it could not be explained by the standard quark-antiquark model.

Fig 1: Illustration of tetraquark

This prompted physicists to speculate that X(3872) could be a Tetraquark, a confined state of two quarks and two antiquarks. Although the exact nature of this particle is still contested, it was the first credible step toward proving the existence of four-quark systems. Since then, several more tetraquark possibilities have been detected in other tests, bolstering the evidence that these unusual states are real.

Discovery of Pentaquarks:

The discovery of Pentaquarks occurred later, but had a greater impact. In 2015, the LHCb Collaboration at CERN announced the discovery of pentaquark states while examining the disintegration of the Lambda baryon. Unlike previous clues, this discovery presented conclusive and statistically significant evidence for a five-quark system. The detected states had features that could only be explained if they were made up of four quarks and one antiquark.

Fig 1: Illustration of pentaquark

Detection and QCD Structure:

Pentaquarks and tetraquarks were not directly observed. Rather, their presence was deduced by the collision of high-energy particles, examination of decay products, and detection of resonance peaks in experimental data. The structure of these exotic hadrons in Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is largely determined by the notion of color confinement.

Multi-quark systems must arrange their internal color charges so that the overall state stays neutral (color-singlet). For example, in tetraquarks, two quarks can unite to produce a diquark in a color antitriplet, while the two antiquarks form an antidiquark in a triplet, resulting in a color-singlet combination. Alternatively, they might exist as two color-neutral mesons loosely bonded together (molecular states). Similarly, pentaquarks can be represented as compact five-quark states or as baryon-meson molecular systems.

Conclusion:

By showing that quarks can combine in ways beyond traditional mesons and baryons, exotic hadrons expand our understanding of the strong force and open new directions for research in particle physics. Each new observation brings us closer to uncovering the deeper principles that govern the universe. Tetraquarks and pentaquarks are a reflection of the universe’s hidden complexity, quietly reminding us that there is always more to explore and understand.

Significant aspects of Fundamental Physics

Saubhik Das
Research Scholar, Gauhati University


Quantum Chromodynamics?

Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is the fundamental physical theory describing the strong nuclear force, which affects elementary subatomic particles called quarks and composite particles (protons, neutrons, mesons) made of quarks.

In quantum chromodynamics (QCD), "chromo" refers to color charge, which is analogous to electric charge in electromagnetism. Just as electric charge is the source of the electromagnetic force, color is the source of the strong force. The term "chromo" in chromodynamics comes from the Greek word for color.

➤ Unlike electromagnetism, QCD posits that force-carrier particles, called gluons, transmit the strong force between particles carrying a strong charge called "color". There are three types of color charge: red, green, and blue, as well as anti-colors. Mixing all three colors produces a neutral particle, such as baryons (protons and neutrons). Antiquarks neutralize a quark's color in mesons.

➤ Key features include color confinement, which prevents isolated quarks from being observed, and asymptotic freedom, where quark interaction weakens at extremely short distances.

➤ Quarks interact by exchanging gluons, which also carry color charges, unlike the photons of quantum electrodynamics. Eight kinds of gluons exist to allow all possible interactions between the three colors of quarks. The interactions between gluons prevent color charges from being pulled apart.

The Quark Model?

The quark model is a classification scheme for hadrons (composite particles made of quarks).

Fig 1: Baryon Structure (Proton and Neutron)

Baryons: Composed of three quarks ($qqq$). Examples: a proton ($uud$) or a neutron ($udd$).

Mesons: Composed of a quark-antiquark pair ($q\bar{q}$).

Color charge: Quarks carry one of three colors (Red, Green, Blue), and combinations must be "colorless" (White).

Quantum Field Theory?

Definition: Quantum Field Theory (QFT) merges quantum mechanics with the laws of physics governing fields, portraying particles such as electrons and photons as excitations of these fields.

Fig 2: Scale of Subatomic Particles

• In theoretical Physics, Quantum field theory (QFT) is a theoretical framework that combines field theory, special relativity and quantum mechanics.

• QFT is used in Particle Physics to construct physical models of sub-atomic particles and in condensed matter physics to construct models of quasiparticles.

• The current Standard Model of particle physics is based on QFT.

• Despite its extraordinary predictive success, QFT faces ongoing challenges in fully incorporating gravity and in establishing a completely rigorous mathematical foundation.

• Its development began in the 1920s with the description of interactions of light and electron, culminating in the first quantum field theory - Quantum Electrodynamics.

Macroscopic Quantum Mechanical Tunnelling and Energy Quantisation in an Electric Circuit

Jitu Moni Kalita
Bsc 3rd year

Introduction:

In classical physics, it is often possible to predict the future behaviour of a system with great accuracy. However, when we examine the universe from a quantum mechanical perspective, such certainty disappears. Instead of definite outcomes, quantum mechanics describes events in terms of probabilities. An event can exist in many possible states until it is observed, at which point only a single outcome becomes real—an effect commonly known as the collapse of the wave function.

In 1924, Louis de Broglie introduced the revolutionary concept of matter waves, proposing that every particle in the universe possesses both particle-like and wave-like behaviour. This dual nature is responsible for a wide range of quantum phenomena that cannot be explained using classical mechanics. Electrons, atoms, and even larger systems display interference, diffraction, and quantisation due to their wave properties.

With advancements in modern technology, physicists are increasingly shifting from classical models to quantum-based approaches. Although the term “quantum” is usually associated with microscopic particles, quantum mechanical behaviour is not limited to the microscopic realm. A remarkable example is the Josephson Junction, a macroscopic device in which electrons tunnel through an insulating barrier. This demonstrates that quantum effects, such as tunnelling and energy quantisation, can appear even in systems visible to the naked eye, bridging the gap between microscopic physics and the macroscopic world.

What is Quantum Tunnelling?

According to classical mechanics, a particle with energy E cannot cross a potential barrier of height V₀ if E < V₀. For example, an electron inside a solid should never escape the surface if its energy is below the barrier potential. Classical theory would predict a strict boundary beyond which the particle cannot exist.

Quantum mechanics, however, tells a very different story. Since particles also behave as waves, their wave functions extend beyond classical boundaries. Even when E < V₀, the wave function does not abruptly drop to zero at the barrier. Instead, it decays gradually inside the barrier, leading to a small but non-zero probability that the particle can appear on the other side. This phenomenon is called quantum tunnelling.

Quantum tunnelling plays a crucial role in numerous natural and technological processes. It enables nuclear fusion inside stars, allows electrons to move between atoms in semiconductors, and forms the operating principle of devices such as Scanning Tunnelling Microscopes (STM), Tunnel Diodes, and Josephson Junctions. Without tunnelling, many of the technologies that shape our modern world simply would not exist.

Macroscopic quantum tunneling :

Quantum tunnelling is also seen on the macroscopic level using a special circuit known as a Josephson Junction. In this junction, two superconductors are placed very close to each other and separated by a thin insulating layer.

A superconductor is a conductor cooled to 0 K (or very close to absolute zero). Between 1924–1925, Albert Einstein and Satyendra Nath Bose predicted that at extremely low temperatures, many bosons occupy the lowest quantum state. Thus, quantum effects such as wave interference become visible on a macroscopic scale.

Condensation refers to a large number of particles occupying one or more specific states. For example, in BCS theory, a superconductor forms when Cooper pairs condense.

If two superconductors are separated by an insulating barrier of width x = a, and taking the left edge of the barrier as origin, the system is divided into three regions:

Region-1: \( x < 0 \)

The Cooper-pair wavefunction in this region is:

\[ \psi_1(x) = \sqrt{n_1}\, e^{i(kx + \theta_1)} \]

\[ k = \frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar} \]

Region-2: \( 0 \le x \le a \)

Inside the insulating potential barrier:

\[ \psi_2(x) = e^{-\kappa x} \]

\[ \kappa = \frac{\sqrt{2m(V_0 - E)}}{\hbar} \]

Region-3: \( x > a \)

The transmitted Cooper-pair wavefunction is:

\[ \psi_3(x) = \sqrt{n_2}\, e^{i(kx + \theta_2)} \]

\[ k = \frac{\sqrt{2mE}}{\hbar} \]

Martinis was awarded by Nobel prize in physics for their experiments demonstrated quantum behavior in electrical circuits large enough to be seen and held, bridging the gap between the microscopic quantum world and macroscopic systems. This discovery has played a key role in the development of quantum technology and quantum computing, allowing physicists to study and apply quantum principles on engineered circuits and devices . Their work builds directly upon earlier ground-breaking research, such as the Josephson effect, and has far reaching implications for both fundamental physics and emerging quantum technologies.

Standard Candles – The Light That Measures the Universe

Alisha Das
Bsc 3rd year

Introduction:

For thousands of years, humans have been captivated by the tiny specks scattered across the night sky. These distant points of light inspired curiosity and wonder, leading people to ask one fundamental question: How far away are the stars? To answer this question, astronomers developed several techniques to measure cosmic distances, one of the most important being the concept of standard candles.

A standard candle is a celestial object or event whose true brightness (absolute luminosity) is known. By comparing its actual brightness with how bright it appears from Earth, astronomers can calculate its distance. This method relies on the inverse square law of light, which states that the intensity of light from a point source decreases in proportion to the square of the distance from that source. In simple terms, as a light source moves farther away, it appears dimmer. By measuring this decrease in brightness, the distance to the object can be determined.

Cepheid Variable Stars as Standard Candles

One of the most reliable standard candles in the universe is the Cepheid variable star. These massive stars undergo regular cycles of expansion and contraction, causing their brightness to vary periodically. Their pulsations resemble a cosmic heartbeat, expanding and contracting in a predictable rhythm.

In 1912, the astronomer Henrietta Swan Leavitt discovered a remarkable relationship between the pulsation period of Cepheid stars and their intrinsic luminosity. She found that stars with longer pulsation periods are intrinsically brighter than those with shorter periods. This discovery, known as the period–luminosity relationship, became one of the most powerful tools for measuring distances to objects far beyond our galaxy.

Later, Edwin Hubble used Cepheid variables to determine the distance to nearby galaxies and demonstrated that the Milky Way is only one among countless galaxies in the universe. His work ultimately led to the discovery that the universe itself is expanding.

Other Types of Standard Candles

To map the structure of our local galactic region, astronomers often use RR Lyrae stars. These stars are smaller and less luminous than Cepheid variables but still provide reliable measurements of distance within the Milky Way. By observing these stars, astronomers can trace the structure and distribution of stars throughout our galaxy.

For exploring even greater distances, astronomers rely on Type Ia supernovae. These extraordinary stellar explosions occur when a white dwarf star reaches a critical mass and undergoes a catastrophic thermonuclear explosion. At their peak brightness, Type Ia supernovae can shine as brightly as an entire galaxy, making them visible across billions of light-years. Because their peak luminosity is nearly uniform, they serve as excellent standard candles for measuring distances on a cosmic scale.

Cosmic Significance of Standard Candles

Type Ia supernovae have played a crucial role in modern cosmology. Observations of these distant explosions revealed that the expansion of the universe is not slowing down as once expected, but is instead accelerating. This surprising discovery led scientists to propose the existence of an unknown force called dark energy, which now appears to dominate the large-scale dynamics of the universe.

The light from standard candles travels enormous distances before reaching Earth. When astronomers observe this light, they are effectively looking back in time, studying events that occurred millions or even billions of years ago. Each photon carries information about the history and evolution of the cosmos.

Conclusion

Standard candles are more than just tools for measuring distance. They act as cosmic storytellers, revealing the structure, scale, and evolution of the universe. By studying their light, astronomers uncover patterns and laws that govern the cosmos. These distant beacons remind us of humanity’s endless curiosity—curiosity that began when someone first gazed at the night sky and asked a simple yet profound question: How far away are those stars?

Physics of the Higgs Boson: The God Particle

Ayush Roy
Bsc 3rd year

Introduction:

Among the most intriguing questions within physics is the following: why does matter have mass? Though we can describe how objects move or interact, the origin of mass remained an enigma until the Higgs boson was discovered, and is often referred to as the "God Particle." The discovery confirmed an important piece of the Standard Model of particle physics and added to our understanding of the universe.

Understanding the Mystery of Mass:

Mass is more than weight; it defines how particles interact with forces and each other. Elementary particles, like electrons and quarks, must have mass to build up atoms, molecules, and eventually stars and planets. For decades, the Standard Model had no explanation for why particles possess mass.

In the 1960s, Peter Higgs and others proposed the idea of the Higgs field, an invisible energy field that fills the universe. As particles move through this field, they experience resistance—similar to a swimmer moving through water. This resistance is what we call mass. Without the Higgs field, particles would remain massless, move at the speed of light, and matter as we know it would not exist.

The Higgs Boson:

The Higgs boson is the particle associated with the Higgs field—a disturbance that confirms its existence. Its discovery required immense effort from the global scientific community, particularly through experiments conducted at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN. This 27-km underground accelerator collides protons at extremely high energies to recreate conditions similar to those just after the Big Bang.

Scientists analyzed vast amounts of data to detect the fleeting signature of the Higgs boson. On July 4, 2012, the ATLAS and CMS collaborations announced the discovery of a new particle consistent with the Higgs boson, marking a historic breakthrough in physics.

Fig 1: Visualization of Higgs boson decay into four muons at the CMS detector, LHC

Importance to Physics:

The Higgs boson is crucial because it explains how particles acquire mass through interaction with the Higgs field. Without it, the Standard Model would be incomplete, and the universe as we know it—composed of matter, stars, and galaxies—could not exist.

Beyond confirming theoretical predictions, the Higgs boson opens new questions in physics. It may provide insights into dark matter, supersymmetry, extra dimensions, and the long-term stability of the universe. Thus, it is not just a discovery, but a gateway to deeper understanding.

Analogy of the Higgs Field:

Imagine walking through a crowded room. People gather around you, slowing your movement. Similarly, particles moving through the Higgs field experience resistance. Some particles, like photons, pass through effortlessly and remain massless, while others interact strongly and gain significant mass. This simple analogy helps explain how mass arises from interaction with a universal field.

Conclusion:

The discovery of the Higgs boson stands as one of the greatest achievements in modern science. It explains the origin of mass, validates the Standard Model, and opens pathways to new physics. Although often called the "God Particle," it represents only a part of the deeper structure of reality and does not imply any theological meaning. Its discovery marks a milestone in humanity’s quest to understand the universe.

Wormholes in the Lab? Quantum Simulation of Exotic Spacetime

Nikitha Guntla
BSc 2nd year


"When Science fiction meets lab reality "

John Mailthot

Introduction:

A "Wormhole" is a theoretical passage that connects two separate regions of spacetime, like a tunnel or bridge through a mountain.In theoretical physics, a wormhole is a solution to Einstein's equation of General Relativity that connects two separate points in spacetime.Think of a tunnel with two mouths, each located at different locations or even different times in the universe. Theoretically, you could enter one mouth and come out the other,skipping vast distances or timelines.
While theoretically possible solutions to physical equations, wormholes have been never observed or detected in the real universe. Wormholes might not just be figments of imagination or products of far future physics anymore. Because Recent advances in quantum computing and simulation suggest that we might be able to model, and even mimic, aspects of wormholes right here in our laboratories. Now before you get too excited about teleporting to another galaxy, here's the catch. Wormholes, as far as we know, are incredibly unstable.They'd collapse almost immediately after forming. Scientists believe that in order to keep one open, you'd need something called exotic matter -a hypothetical type of matter with negative energy density and pressure. That kind of stuff hasn't been discovered yet.So for now, real wormhole travel is still off the table.

Baby Wormhole on a Quantum Computer:

In 2022, researchers from Caltech, Google, Fermilab, and others made headlines by using Google's quantum computer, Sycamore, to simulate a( toy model of a wormhole) kind of baby wormhole .While no real holes in spacetime were created, this experiment marked a major milestone in the field of quantum simulation and theoretical physics.To be clear, they didn't create a real wormhole. But they simulated a simplified version of one.The team used a theoretical model known as the Sachdev Ye Kitaev (SYK) system, which has deep connections to black hole physics and quantum gravity.In their setup, they encoded a bit of quantum information a qubit into one part of an entangled quantum system. That qubid later reappeared in another part, simulating how information might transverse a wormhole. Although this resembles quantum teleportation, the experiment was also inspired by a theoretical idea called the ER=EPR conjecture. EP=EPR is a theory that suggests wormholes (Einstein Rosen bridges, ER) and quantum entanglement (EPR-named after Einstein ,Podolsky, and Rosen again)might actually be two sides of the same coin. Basically, it says if two parts are entangled, they might be connected by a sort of microscopic wormhole. The experiment with Sycamore didn't prove this ,but it did demonstrated that quantum computers can simulate it's predicted behavior, offering a rare and valuable opportunity to test aspects of quantum gravity in a lab setting. That's groundbreaking, considering that quantum gravity -an attempt to unify gravity with quantum mechanics remains one of the biggest unsolved puzzles in physics.

Why Quantum Wormhole Simulations Matter?:

Firstly, quantum simulations like this bridge gap between quantum mechanics( which exlains particles and forces on the smallest scales) and general relativity (which explains gravity), two fundamental theories that currently don't align. If we can simulate aspects of gravity using controllable quantum systems, we may begin to test and refine theories that have previously existed only on chalkboards and in abstract mathematics.

Secondly, these experiments showcase the evolving power of quantum computers. These simulations are a proof of concept that quantum computers can be used for more than just factoring large numbers or optimizing logistics. Once seen mainly as tools for code breaking or solving optimization problems, quantum devices are now stepping into the realm of fundamental physics helping Scientists explore the nature of spacetime itself.

Limits and Misconceptions: No Real Spaceships Yet :

The experiment was conducted with only a few qubits and was based on a highly simplified model. Real spacetime is vastly more complex, and again, just to be super clear: we didn't open a wormhole. We didn't send a scapeship or even a photon across the galaxy. We sent a qubit through a simulation. But still baby steps. Many physicists caution that While the analogy between quantum teleportation and wormholes is mathematically intriguing, it may not generalize to realistic physical scenarios. In short, we're exploring shadows of wormholes not the real thing.

Fusion Energy: The Quest for a Star on The Earth

Pranab Debnath
BSc 1st sem


Introduction:

The dream of harnessing the power of the stars has fascinated scientists for decades. Deep inside the sun and other stars, a natural process called nuclear fusion releases an immense amount of energy, sustaining their light and heat for billions of years. If humans could replicate even a fraction of that process on Earth, it would open the door to virtually limitless, clean, and safe energy—solving one of the greatest challenges of our time. This ambition is often referred to as the quest for a star on Earth.

What is Fusion Energy?

Fusion energy is produced when two light atomic nuclei combine to form a heavier nucleus, releasing vast amounts of energy in the process. In stars, hydrogen nuclei (protons) fuse to form helium through a series of reactions under extreme temperature and pressure. The difference in mass between the initial and final particles is converted into energy, according to Einstein's famous equation $E=mc^{2}$.

Fig 1: The Nuclear Fusion Process

On Earth, scientists aim to replicate a similar process using isotopes of hydrogen, mainly deuterium and tritium. When these two isotopes fuse, they form helium and a high-energy neutron, releasing about 17.6 million electron volts (MeV) of energy per reaction. If controlled properly, this energy could power turbines and generate electricity without the harmful byproducts associated with fossil fuels or nuclear fission.

Importance of Fusion Energy:

The world's growing energy demands and the climate crisis have made the search for sustainable energy sources more urgent than ever. Fusion offers several remarkable advantages:

  • Abundant Fuel Supply: Deuterium can be extracted from seawater, and tritium can be bred from lithium; both are widely available. This means fusion fuel could last for millions of years.

  • Clean and Safe Energy: Fusion produces no carbon dioxide or long-lived radioactive waste. Unlike fission, there is no risk of a runaway chain reaction or nuclear meltdown.

  • High Energy Output: Fusion releases about four million times more energy than burning coal or oil and four times more than nuclear fission for the same mass of fuel.

  • Global Energy Security: Because fuel sources are distributed worldwide, fusion could reduce geopolitical tensions linked to fossil fuel dependency.

The Challenge of Control:

Recreating the conditions of the sun on Earth is no easy task. To achieve fusion, scientists must heat hydrogen fuel to temperatures often exceeding 100 million degrees Celsius and confine it long enough for nuclei to collide. The main difficulty lies in containing this ultra-hot plasma; no material container can withstand such heat, so scientists use magnetic or inertial confinement methods.

Confinement Methods:

The most advanced approach is Magnetic Confinement Fusion (MCF), particularly in a doughnut-shaped device known as a tokamak. The ITER project in France aims to demonstrate a tenfold energy gain using this method. Alternatively, Inertial Confinement Fusion (ICF) uses intense lasers to compress fuel pellets, a method that recently achieved "ignition" at the National Ignition Facility (NIF) in the United States.

Conclusion:

The quest for fusion energy is humanity's bold attempt to bring a miniature sun to Earth. While engineering challenges regarding materials and tritium handling remain, the scientific foundation is now solid. If successful, fusion will stand as one of humanity's greatest achievements, providing an inexhaustible source of clean electricity for millennia to come.

Space-time : The four dimensions universe

Sunil Kumar Paswan
BSc 1st year


Introduction:

If we want to search deep in the space,the basic physics is applied to all across the universe is concept of space-time . space and time seem like two completely different things. Space describes about the area where an object or anything could be present ,it has dimensions such as length , breath and height , on the other hand time, tells us when things happen: the past, present, or future. For centuries, scientists believed that space and time were completely separate, absolute entities-with a universal clock ticking everywhere in the universe at the same rate, and an unchanging, rigid stage where the events of our universe play out. But this view changed forever in the early 20th century, thanks to the revolutionary work of Albert Einstein. Einstein showed that space and time are not independent-they are deeply connected, and together form a single, four-dimensional continuum: “space-time"
Before Einstein’s work, the prevailing view in physics was that light, as a wave, must travel through a medium called the “luminiferous aether”. Einstein’s theory of special relativity
eliminated the need for this concept, while his theory of general relativity offered a radically new understanding of gravity.

Why space-time introduced :

The need to unite space and time came from odd results in physics experiments about motion and light. For example, the famous Michelson-Morley experiment showed that the speed of light is always the same-no matter how an observer moves relative to the light source. This was shocking, and it broke the rules of classical physics . To explain this, Einstein proposed that measurements of distance and time depend on the observer's motion. Two people moving at different speeds could disagree about how far apart two events are, or how much time passes between them. However, everyone would agree on a single quantity, combining space and time- what's now called the "space-time interval." This new theory merged three dimensions of space and one of time into a four-dimensional framework.
The Building Blocks: Events and the Interval In everyday life, we describe the position of an object using three numbers (for example, coordinates x, y, z). To completely specify an event (like a thunderclap or lightning strike), we also need to state when it happened (t). In space time, every “event” is described by four coordinates: (x, y, z, t). But these coordinates alone are not enough. Different observers, moving relative to one another, will assign different coordinates to the same event. What stays constant is the “interval” between two events,calculated by: (Δs)² = (cΔt)² – (∆x)² - (Δy)² - (Δz)²Here, c is the speed of light. This quantity, the space-time interval, is the same for all observers and forms the foundation for Einstein’s theory of relativity.

New Picture: Relativity and The ‘Fabric’ of Reality :

Einstein's ideas created a new way to look at gravity. Earlier, Isaac Newton pictured gravity as a "force" pulling toward each other across space. Einstein suggested something more subtle and beautiful: massive objects, like planets and stars, actually bend and curve the fabric of space-time. What we feel as gravity is just objects following the curves in this fabric. This idea is called the "general theory of relativity," and it has been confirmed by experiments, such as the bending of light near the sun or the detection of gravitational waves.
A common analogy is to imagine space-time as a stretched rubber sheet. Placing a heavy ball (a planet or star) on the sheet creates a dip, curving the surface. Marbles rolling nearby will curve around the dip, just as Earth orbits the sun-not because of a mysterious force, but because the sun has curved space-time around it.

Fig 1: Bending of space febric due to heavy planet

Light Cones, World-Lines, and Causality

Physicists use "light cones" to visualize space-time. Imagine an event (like a firecracker explosion): the light from this event spreads out in all directions at speed c, forming a cone in space-time diagrams. Everything inside the "future light cone" can potentially be influenced by this event; everything inside the "past light cone" could have affected it. Lines drawn through space-time to represent the path of an object are called "world-lines." Every particle or person traces out a unique world-line as they move through the universe.

The Big Picture: Black Holes, Cosmology, and Beyond :

Space-time is central to some of the most fascinating questions in physics: Black Holes: These mysterious objects are regions where space-time is so fiercely curved that nothing, not even light, can escape. The Expanding Universe: The entire universe is described as a vast, "stretching" space-time, with galaxies moving apart as space itself expands. Gravitational Waves: Ripples in space-time, created by violent astrophysical events, were first observed in 2015-confirming Einstein's prediction a century earlier. Contemporary research is probing the very structure of space-time on the smallest scales, searching for a quantum theory of gravity and new, deeper laws.

Conclusion:

Space-time is not just a concept of physics it is the fabric of universe that exists in every part of universe ,it is often referred as fabric of reality and visualised as four dimensional block ,it describes anything happens in the universe along the propagation of electro magnetic waves such as light . It challenges our common-sense ideas, unites gravity and motion, and is central to both our understanding of the cosmos and the technology we use each day.

Electricity: The Lifeline of Modern Civilization

Fariha Ahmed
BSc 1st year


Introduction:

Electricity is one of the most powerful and essential discoveries in human history. It is the invisible force that lights up our homes powers industries, drives communication and fuels modern technology. From the moment we wake up and switch on a light bulb to the time we charge our phones before going to bed, electricity plays a crucial role in our daily lives. Without it, the world as we know it would come to a standstill. This article explores the nature of electricity, its generation, uses, importance, and challenges in the modern world.

What is Electricity? :

Electricity is a form of energy resulting from the movement of charged particles, mainly electrons, through a conductor such as a wire. It can be static, like the spark we fee after rubbing certain materials, or current electricity, which flows continuously through circuits and is used for practical purposes Though electricity has existed in nature for millions of years in the form of lightning or electric eels, humans only began to understand and harness it in the last few centuries. Scientists like Benjamin Franklin, Alessandro Volta, Michael Faraday, and Thomas Edison made significant contributions that transformed electricity from a natural phenomenon into a practical utility.

Fig 1: Simple circuit

There are two main types of electricity:

  1. Electricity :This is the buildup of electric charge on the surface of an object. You’ve likely experienced static electricity when you rub a balloon on your hair or walk across a carpet and then touch a metal doorknob.

  2. Current Electricity : This is the flow of electric charge through a conductor. It is the form of electricity that powers our homes and devices.

How Electricity Is Generated:

Electricity is not a primary energy source; it must be generated using other sources of energy. Most electricity is produced in power plants where mechanical energy is converted into electrical energy using generators. The primary sources of energy for electricity generation include:

  1. Fossil Fuels (Coal, Oil, Natural Gas):These are burned to produce steam, which drives turbines connected to generators.

  2. Nuclear Energy: In nuclear power plants, nuclear reactions release heat, which also produces steam for turbines.

  3. Renewable Energy Sources:
    • Hydropower : Uses flowing water to spin turbines.

    • Wind Energy :Wind turns the blades of wind turbines to generate electricity.

    • Solar Power : Photovoltaic cells convert sunlight directly into electricity.

    • Geothermal Energy Uses heat from beneath the Earth’s surface.

With growing concerns about pollution and climate change, there is an increasing shift towards cleaner and renewable energy sources.

Transmission and Distribution:

Once electricity is generated, it must be transported to where it’s needed. This is done through a complex system known as the electrical grid. Here’s how it works:

  1. Generation:Electricity is produced at power stations.

  2. Transmission: High-voltage power lines carry electricity over long distances

  3. Distribution: Substations reduce the voltage, and distribution lines deliver electricity to homes, schools, and businesses.

Maintaining a stable supply of electricity requires a balance between demand (how much electricity people are using) and supply (how much is being generated). This system is managed in real time by utility companies.

Uses of Electricity:

Electricity has countless uses in modern life. Some of the most common include:

  • Lighting :Electric lights brighten homes, streets, and cities.

  • Heating and Cooling :Electric heaters, air conditioners, and refrigerators help maintain comfortable living conditions.

  • Communication :Devices like phones, computers, and the internet all rely on electricity.

  • Industry :Factories and workshops use electricity to power machines and tools.

The convenience and versatility of electricity have made it indispensable to both economic development and personal comfort.

The Future of Electricity:

As the global population grows and technology advances, the demand for electricity continues to rise. At the same time, there's a growing push to reduce carbon emissions and combat climate change. This means the future of electricity will involve:

  1. Cleaner Energy Sources : Expanding the use of solar, wind, and other renewable sources.

  2. Smart Grids Upgrading the electrical grid to be more efficient and reliable using digital technology.

  3. Energy Storage Improving battery technology to store electricity for use when generation is low.

  4. Electrification of Transport Replacing gasoline-powered vehicles with electric ones.

  5. Decentralized Generation Homes and businesses generating their own electricity using solar panels and other local systems.

These trends promise a more sustainable and energy efficient future.

Conclusion:

Electricity is a remarkable form of energy that powers the modern world. From its basic principles to its complex infrastructure, electricity enables much of what we take for granted. As we look to the future, balancing our growing energy needs with environmental sustainability will be one of the great challenges—and opportunities of our time. Understanding electricity is the first step toward using it wisely and responsibly.
